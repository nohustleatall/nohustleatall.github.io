Appliance notes

A 12-hour streaming data appliance on Orange Pi RV2

The Orange Pi RV2 is not impressive as a general scalar CPU, but it is credible as a low-power streaming aggregate appliance. A 12-hour run proved stability, and a later combined run added Bluesky plus crypto order books without getting close to saturating the board.

The useful result

The important result is not that Nostr mentions predict crypto prices. They do not, at least not from naive counts. The useful result is that the board can comfortably reduce many live public streams into compact rolling features. For this workload, the hard part becomes signal design rather than ingest capacity.

What the appliance does

The prototype subscribes to public streams, discards raw messages quickly, keeps rolling counters and baselines, writes compact aggregate rows, and exposes a small read-only dashboard.

  • Eight Nostr relays.
  • Coinbase and Kraken crypto trade streams.
  • Optional Coinbase and Kraken order-book aggregate buckets.
  • Optional Bluesky Jetstream aggregate buckets.
  • SQLite WAL persistence with aggregate-only rows.
  • No raw posts, raw trade messages, or pubkeys stored.
  • Rolling term counts and unique-author counts.
  • Baseline ratio, velocity, acceleration, z-score, and quality score.
  • Suppression for high-count low-diversity noise.
  • BTC/ETH/SOL topic co-mentions and hourly rollups.
  • A dashboard and CLI summary helper for inspection.

Signal quality was the hard part

BTC was extremely dense and noisy. ETH and SOL were lower-volume but produced cleaner relative spike scores. Raw mention count alone was not useful.

Symbol Mentions Author-bucket sum Max bucket mentions Max quality score Trades
BTCUSD 439,639 31,567 1,175 0.88 278,154
ETHUSD 11,393 897 130 17.80 199,431

The scoring layer matters because high raw counts can come from repeated templates, domains, or single-source noise. Diversity-adjusted scoring and suppression made smaller ETH/SOL events visible instead of letting BTC volume dominate every view.

The combined stress run

The later scale test turned on the useful public sources together: Nostr relays, Bluesky Jetstream, Coinbase trades, Coinbase order books, Kraken order books, aggregate SQLite persistence, and the dashboard. It ran for 30 minutes with one-minute buckets.

Why this is an RV2-shaped workload

This is mostly network I/O, JSON parsing, counters, rolling statistics, order-book aggregation, and compact SQLite writes. It does not need a GPU, NPU, or large local language model. It needs steady ingest, bounded memory, compact persistence, and enough CPU for simple feature logic.

That fits the RV2 better than many of the workloads where it lost to the Pi 400. It is an always-on reducer: many public streams in, aggregate features out.

Dashboard and inspection

The dashboard is deliberately small and framework-free. It reads aggregate SQLite tables and shows latest bucket counts, recent scored alerts, BTC/ETH/SOL panels, top quality-scored terms, suppressed noisy terms, relay health, hourly rollups, and topic co-mentions.

That matters operationally. A headless appliance still needs a way to answer: is it alive, which relays are healthy, how fast is data arriving, and what is being suppressed?

What this is not

This is not a trading strategy. The Nostr/Coinbase experiments showed that naive social mention counts are noisy. The largest first-pass correlations were not enough to treat as market signal, and the better 12-hour appliance output is still heuristic.

The current scoring uses baseline ratio, z-score, velocity, acceleration, author diversity, mentions per author, and suppression for low-diversity repeated sources. That is useful for exploration, but it is not statistically validated.

The aggregate-only design is also a tradeoff. It is good for privacy and storage, but it means later forensic analysis cannot inspect raw posts that caused an alert.

Practical conclusion

This is the strongest real appliance result so far. The RV2 ran a multi-stream public data reducer for 12 hours with low CPU, low memory, compact storage, live dashboard serving, and enough headroom for more feature logic. Then it handled a denser combined Nostr, Bluesky, trade, and order-book mix at only 0.18 of one core.

The next step is not proving ingest again. It is making the appliance production-shaped: configuration, service wrapper, retention policy, alert sinks, dashboard authentication for non-LAN use, and longer unattended runs.

No analytics, comments, or tracking scripts are used here.
